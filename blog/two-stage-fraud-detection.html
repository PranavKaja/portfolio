Two-Stage Fraud Detection: XGBoost for Speed, an LLM for Reasoning

A classical model is great at the bulk of cases. A reasoning layer on top catches what it misses, and tells the analyst why.

Most fraud systems hand the analyst a number. The model scores a transaction 0.87 and the analyst has to figure out, from scratch, why it looks suspicious and what to do about it. That's fine at low volume and brutal at scale. Card networks see millions of transactions a day, under 1% of them fraud, each one real money.

So I built a two-stage pipeline that splits the job between two systems that are each good at different things.

Stage 1: XGBoost scores everything

A gradient-boosted model trained on 284,807 historical card transactions scores every transaction with a fraud probability. It's fast and cheap, so it scales to the full firehose and flags only the highest-risk handful for review.

Stage 2: an LLM reasons about the few

Each flagged transaction goes to a Google Gemini triage agent that reads the raw feature values and writes a structured case file in a fixed JSON shape. It gives the verdict, the confidence, the primary signals, the reasoning, and a recommended action:

{ "verdict": "block", "confidence": 0.92, "primary_signals": ["V14","V10","V12"], "reasoning": "Multiple anomalous patterns coincide with an elevated model score, consistent with card-testing fraud.", "recommended_action": "Block and trigger SMS verification." }

The analyst reviews a decision instead of a bare number. What used to take ~5 minutes of investigation per case becomes ~30 seconds of verification. And because the LLM only sees the small slice the model already flagged, you only pay its cost where it earns its keep.

The case that justified the whole thing

During testing, one transaction scored 0.000000. The model was certain it was legitimate. It was actually fraud. The model would have let it through. The LLM, reading the raw features rather than the model's confidence, noticed several anomalous signals and flagged it anyway.

That's the entire argument for two layers: the model is excellent on the bulk of cases but has occasional blind spots, and a second opinion that reasons differently sometimes catches what it missed.

The engineering arc

It didn't start clean. A vanilla gradient-boosting baseline stalled at 0.60 AUC, barely better than a coin flip. The cause wasn't the algorithm. It was the data: a 577-to-1 class imbalance was drowning the loss function, and 1,081 duplicate rows pushed it toward bimodal predictions that broke the metric.

  • Switching to XGBoost with scale_pos_weight=577 told the model to weight fraud examples 577× during training. L2 regularization smoothed the duplicate-row impact. AUC jumped from 0.60 to 0.978, at 84% recall and 84% precision on the rare class.
  • The LLM layer enforces JSON with response_mime_type="application/json". No regex parsing, no markdown fences to strip.
  • Only the top 5 most anomalous features go in the prompt, not all 30. Focused prompts, lower tokens.
  • A rate-limited batch processor handles 100 transactions in ~10 minutes with caching, so a dropped session loses no work, and projects cost across 1K to 1M daily volumes (~$30/month at 100K/day).

Why it matters

The interesting part isn't the AUC. It's the shape. This is how major payments teams are starting to deploy LLMs internally: as a reasoning layer on top of traditional ML, not as a replacement. The model gives you speed. The LLM gives you explainability and an audit trail. You don't have to pick.
